\begin{abstract}
Dengue Hemorrhagic Fever (DHF) remains a major public health problem in tropical and subtropical regions, making rapid and accurate diagnosis crucial to reduce complications and mortality. Clinical datasets used for DHF classification contain various numerical features with varying value ranges, which can impact the performance of deep learning models. Although data normalization has been widely used in preprocessing, studies comparing the effectiveness of various normalization techniques for DHF classification are limited. This study analyzes the effect of three normalization approaches—no normalization, StandardScaler, and MinMaxScaler—on the performance of a Deep Neural Network (DNN) model using two dataset configurations: a complete dataset containing clinical symptoms and laboratory test results, and a dataset containing only clinical symptoms for an initial screening scenario. Model performance was evaluated using Stratified 5-Fold Cross Validation, and the final model was tested on a separate test dataset. The results showed that data normalization significantly improved classification performance. Among the evaluated methods, StandardScaler performed best on the full dataset with an accuracy of 96.96\%, an F1-score of 97.00\%, and an ROC-AUC of 0.9972. These findings suggest that selecting the right normalization technique can improve the accuracy, reliability, and stability of deep learning-based dengue fever classification models in processing heterogeneous clinical data.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Dengue Hemorrhagic Fever (DHF) remains a major health challenge, particularly for people in tropical and subtropical countries. This disease is transmitted through the bite of the Aedes aegypti mosquito, and the number of cases has continued to increase over the past two decades. According to the World Health Organization (WHO), an estimated 100–400 million cases of dengue fever occur annually worldwide, with Southeast Asia and Latin America being the most affected regions. \cite{b1}. Given that this disease can progress rapidly and cause serious complications, early and accurate diagnosis is crucial for improving patient success and reducing mortality. \cite{b2,b3}

In clinical practice, the diagnosis of dengue fever is generally based on a combination of patient symptoms and laboratory findings, such as platelet count, leukocyte count, hematocrit, and hemoglobin levels \cite{b4}. However, these clinical manifestations often overlap with other febrile illnesses, making diagnosis very difficult in the early stages of infection. To support clinical decision-making, Artificial Intelligence (AI), particularly Machine Learning (ML) and Deep Learning (DL), are increasingly being adopted for medical classification tasks due to their ability to identify complex relationships in healthcare data \cite{b5,b6}. Among these approaches, Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) have demonstrated strong performance in classifying structured clinical data \cite{b7,b8,b9}.

Despite these advances, the effectiveness of deep learning models is significantly affected by the quality of data preprocessing. Clinical datasets typically include heterogeneous numeric features with varying value ranges, which can slow model convergence and reduce predictive performance if not scaled \cite{b10}. Although feature normalization has been widely incorporated into previous dengue classification studies, relatively few have systematically compared different normalization techniques under multiple dataset configurations. To address this gap, this study evaluates three normalization strategies—no normalization, \textit{StandardScaler}, and \textit{MinMaxScaler}—using DNNs trained on two dataset configurations: a full dataset containing clinical symptoms and laboratory results, and a symptom-only dataset. Model robustness and generalization were assessed using five-fold cross-validation, while classification performance was measured using Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1-Score, and ROC-AUC \cite{b11}.

\section{Related Work}

Previous research has shown that both Machine Learning (ML) and Deep Learning (DL) are effective approaches for dengue classification. A systematic review by Andrade Girón et al. /cite{b12} highlighted the successful application of algorithms such as Artificial Neural Networks (ANN), Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN), Random Forest, Support Vector Machine (SVM), and Extreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost). The review also emphasized that data preprocessing, particularly feature normalization and K-Fold Cross Validation, plays a key role in improving classification performance and model generalization. Similarly, previous research has shown that normalization methods, including Z-score and Min-Max Scaling, along with cross validation, contribute to higher classification accuracy and more reliable model performance \cite{b13,b17,b18,b20,b22}.

Several dengue classification studies have reported accuracy above 97\% using ANN and SVM models with appropriate preprocessing and validation strategies \cite{b14,b15,b16,b19}. Other comparative studies achieved accuracy exceeding 99\% using feature normalization combined with machine learning algorithms such as Extra Tree Classifier and Decision Tree \cite{b3,b24}. Similar findings have also been reported in Indonesia, where Min-Max Scaling improved the performance of an XGBoost-based dengue classification model \cite{b5}. Overall, these studies show that feature scaling and validation strategies substantially impact model accuracy and generalization \cite{b21}.

Although previous research has demonstrated the benefits of normalization, most studies have only evaluated one preprocessing technique or one dataset configuration. Comprehensive comparisons of different normalization methods for DNN-based dengue classification across different dataset configurations are rare. To address this limitation, this study systematically compares three normalization strategies using complete clinical-laboratory data and symptom-only datasets.

This dataset consists of 1,141 patient medical records collected from Permata Gading Clinic between January 2024 and December 2025. The class distribution is relatively balanced, consisting of 575 non-dengue cases and 566 dengue cases; therefore, no additional resampling techniques, such as SMOTE, were applied. Normalization was performed only on continuous numeric features, including age, body temperature, systolic and diastolic blood pressure, laboratory measurements, and fever duration. Binary variables representing clinical symptoms, gender, and target label were excluded from the normalization process because their values already represent categorical information.

\subsection{Data Preprocessing}

Data preprocessing was performed to improve data quality before model training. Duplicate data was removed, while missing values for numeric and categorical attributes were filled using the median input method. Next, categorical variables, such as gender, clinical symptoms, and diagnostic labels, were converted to binary values for processing by the Deep Neural Network (DNN) model. All 21 features were retained due to their clinical relevance to dengue diagnosis.

After preprocessing, the dataset was split into training-validation and testing data with an 80:20 ratio using stratified sampling. The training process was performed using five-fold stratified cross-validation to evaluate the model's stability and generalization ability. Next, the final model was retrained using the entire training-validation data with an 85:15 internal split for early stopping. It was then evaluated on a held-out test set to obtain more objective results and minimize selection bias.

Fig.~\ref{fig_norm_framework} illustrates the experimental framework adopted in this study. Two dataset configurations were evaluated: the complete dataset, which includes both clinical symptoms and laboratory examination results, and the symptoms-only dataset, which excludes laboratory features to simulate an early screening scenario. Each dataset configuration was tested using three normalization strategies, namely no normalization, \textit{StandardScaler}, and \textit{MinMaxScaler}, resulting in six experimental scenarios.

\textit{StandardScaler} standardizes numerical features by transforming them to have zero mean and unit variance,

\begin{equation}
z=\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma},
\end{equation}

where $\mu$ and $\sigma$ represent the mean and standard deviation of each feature, respectively \cite{b26}. Meanwhile, \textit{MinMaxScaler} rescales feature values into the range of $[0,1]$ using

\begin{equation}
x'=\frac{x-x_{min}}{x_{max}-x_{min}},
\end{equation}

where $x_{min}$ and $x_{max}$ denote the minimum and maximum values of each feature \cite{b27}. The performance of each experimental scenario was subsequently compared to determine the most effective normalization strategy for DNN-based dengue fever classification.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

The performance of the proposed DNN model was evaluated using several widely adopted classification metrics, including Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1-Score, and the Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve (ROC-AUC) \cite{b34,b35}. Accuracy measures the overall proportion of correctly classified instances and is calculated as

\begin{equation}
Accuracy = \frac{TP + TN}{TP + TN + FP + FN}
\end{equation}

Precision and Recall were used to assess the model's ability to correctly identify positive dengue cases while minimizing false predictions, and are defined as

\begin{equation}
Precision = \frac{TP}{TP + FP}, \qquad
Recall = \frac{TP}{TP + FN}
\end{equation}

The F1-Score represents the harmonic mean of Precision and Recall, providing a balanced measure of classification performance, particularly when both false positives and false negatives are important.

\begin{equation}
F1 = 2 \times \frac{Precision \times Recall}{Precision + Recall}
\end{equation}

In these equations, \textit{True Positive} (TP) and \textit{True Negative} (TN) denote correctly classified dengue fever and non-dengue fever cases, respectively, whereas \textit{False Positive} (FP) and \textit{False Negative} (FN) represent incorrect classifications. In addition to these metrics, ROC-AUC was used to evaluate the model's discrimination capability across different decision thresholds. Confusion Matrix analysis was also performed to examine the distribution of correct and incorrect predictions and to compare the classification performance obtained from each normalization strategy and dataset configuration.

\section{Discussion}

The experimental results demonstrate that data normalization plays an important role in improving the performance of Deep Neural Network (DNN) models for Dengue Fever classification. Among the evaluated methods, \textit{StandardScaler} consistently produced the highest performance across all evaluation metrics. This result indicates that standardizing numerical features to zero mean and unit variance allows the DNN to learn more effectively from clinical data with different value ranges, particularly laboratory measurements such as platelet count, leukocyte count, hematocrit, and hemoglobin. In contrast, models trained without normalization produced substantially lower performance, confirming that differences in feature scales negatively affect the optimization process.

The comparison between dataset configurations also highlights the importance of laboratory examination results. Models trained using the complete dataset consistently outperformed those using only symptom-based features. This finding suggests that laboratory indicators provide additional diagnostic information that complements clinical symptoms, leading to more accurate classification. Nevertheless, the symptoms-only configuration still achieved satisfactory performance above 93\%, demonstrating its potential for preliminary screening when laboratory results are not yet available.

The training and validation curves, together with the cross-validation results, indicate that the proposed DNN generalized well to unseen data. The small difference between training and validation performance suggests that the model learned representative patterns without experiencing significant overfitting.

\subsection{Limitations}

This study has several limitations. First, the proposed DNN model was not compared with conventional machine learning algorithms such as Support Vector Machine (SVM), Random Forest, or XGBoost, limiting broader performance comparison. Second, the decision threshold of 0.4 was selected empirically to prioritize recall for clinical screening rather than through systematic threshold optimization. Finally, the dataset was obtained from a single data source with a relatively limited number of patient records. Therefore, further validation using larger and more diverse datasets from multiple healthcare institutions is required to evaluate the robustness and generalizability of the proposed framework.

\section{Conclusion}

This study investigated the effect of different normalization techniques on the performance of a Deep Neural Network for Dengue Fever classification using clinical symptoms and laboratory data. Experimental results showed that normalization significantly improved classification performance compared to using raw data. Among the evaluated approaches, \textit{StandardScaler} achieved the best overall performance, achieving the highest Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1-Score, and ROC-AUC. Furthermore, models trained using the full dataset consistently outperformed models using only symptom-based features, indicating that laboratory measurements provide valuable information for disease classification.

The use of nested 5-Fold Cross-Validation, followed by retraining the final model on the full training dataset, also demonstrated stable model performance and good generalization ability. These findings indicate that the proposed framework has strong potential to support early Dengue Fever screening and aid clinical decision-making.

\section{Future Work}

Future research should evaluate the proposed framework using a larger, multi-center clinical dataset to further validate its robustness. Comparison with conventional machine learning algorithms and more sophisticated deep learning architectures would also provide a broader assessment of the model's performance. Furthermore, integrating the proposed model into a real-time, web-based clinical decision support system would facilitate its practical implementation in healthcare settings.
